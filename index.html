Tug of Paw

Dog Exercise & Entertainment System

The Market Challenge

During the COVID-19 pandemic, UK pet adoptions surged. As owners returned to the office, dogs were left isolated for longer periods, leading to disruptive behaviours. Existing pulling toys on the market all require a human to be on the other end, providing the resistance for the dog to counter. There is no available self-automated tug-of-war system on the market. The aim was to design a mechanically adjustable device capable of independent, prolonged engagement while safely withstanding the dynamic loads of a lunging dog.

Mechanical Solution

The 'Tug of Paw' is a wall-mounted mechanical system driven by a 12mm En8 steel shaft. To allow for independent play, the system utilises a torsional clock spring that stores potential energy when the dog pulls the dynamic climbing rope, automatically reeling the rope back in upon release.

To accommodate dogs of varying strengths, pulling resistance is generated by a mechanical friction brake clamped over a custom steel disc. Crucially, a CSK12PP Sprag Clutch (one-way bearing) is integrated into the shaft. This ensures the friction brake only applies resistance when the dog pulls, allowing the spring to retract the rope freely without fighting the brake's friction.

FEA & Iterative Optimization

Following the Pahl and Beitz design methodology, rigorous FEA was conducted based on a 2000N dynamic load (simulating a 40kg dog lunging at 5m/s).

Brake Disc: To ensure the spring could successfully retract the rope, the rotational inertia of the shaft components had to be minimised. Through topological optimisation, the brake disc's mass was reduced by 40% (550g to 332g), decreasing its moment of inertia by 37% while maintaining a minimum safety factor of 4.6.

Modular Hook: A custom carabiner-style hook was developed to allow owners to swap out toys. Initial FEA identified peak stresses of 26 GPa at the mounting hole. Through shape optimization, material was removed from low-stress regions and load distribution was improved, reducing base stress by 87%.

Technical Specifications

  • Design Method Pahl & Beitz
  • Nominal Dynamic Load 2000N
  • Primary Shaft 12mm En8 Steel
  • Retraction Torsional Spring
  • Resistance Friction Brake
  • Directional Control Sprag Clutch
  • Testing Fusion 360

DFM & Cost Savings

Early iterations were over-engineered with a 30mm shaft. By scaling the shaft down to 12mm after force calculations, bearing and clutch costs were reduced significantly, saving ~17% of the total project budget which was reallocated to higher-grade steel housing.
